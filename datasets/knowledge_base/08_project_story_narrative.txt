CHAPTER 1 — THE BEGINNING (July 2025)
======================================

On 15 July 2025, NxtGen Cloud Infrastructure Ltd awarded the EPC contract for their
flagship Tier IV Hyperscale Data Centre to Tata Projects Limited. The contract was
worth INR 487 Crores. The project — code-named HYDC-01 — was to be constructed at
Devanahalli Industrial Area, north of Bengaluru, close to the Kempegowda International
Airport corridor that had become India's most important data centre belt.

Vikram Sharma, a seasoned data centre project manager who had previously delivered
two Tier III projects for global hyperscalers, was appointed Project Manager.
Priya Nair, the regional Project Director, took overall accountability.
The client's representative, Arjun Kapoor, had a personal deadline: the first
colocation customer — a major Indian bank — had signed a 12 MW reservation agreement
with a go-live commitment of 1 September 2026.

That date would drive every decision for the next twelve months.

Design was already at 60% completion when the contract was awarded, thanks to a
parallel fast-track design engagement with Jacobs India led by Rahul Desai (electrical)
and Anita Pillai (mechanical). Site possession was granted on 28 July 2025.
L&T C&B mobilised within days.

The first major procurement packages went to tender in August 2025:
- PKG-EL-001: 11 kV Medium Voltage Switchgear — tendered to Schneider Electric India and Siemens India
- PKG-EL-002: UPS System (8 × 1.6 MW modules) — tendered to PowerGrid Systems and ABB Power Protection
- PKG-EL-003: Diesel Generators (4 × 3.5 MW) — tendered to Cummins India and Caterpillar India

CHAPTER 2 — THE FIRST ALARM BELL (September–October 2025)
==========================================================

On 12 September 2025, Tata Projects received a commercial offer from PowerGrid Systems
Pvt Ltd for Package PKG-EL-002 (UPS). The offer was attractive on price — INR 34.7 Crores,
exactly at budget. But buried on page 3 of the quotation was a technical note that would
cause months of disruption:

"The proposed UPS configuration is N (parallel) for the connected IT load. Redundant
(N+1) module can be added in Phase 1B."

Aditya Singh, Procurement Manager, did not initially recognise the significance of this
statement. He forwarded the quotation to Rahul Desai at Jacobs India for technical review.

Rahul Desai's response was immediate and unambiguous. He raised RFI-EL-001 on 8 October 2025:

"N+1 redundancy is a mandatory Day 1 requirement for Phase 1A. There is no provision
for deferral to Phase 1B. Any vendor proposing N-only configuration must be formally rejected."

Dr. Meera Iyer at NxtGen Cloud confirmed this position within hours. The client had
committed to a Tier IV data centre. Tier IV requires fault tolerance. N-only UPS was
not fault tolerant. The answer was non-negotiable.

Aditya Singh contacted PowerGrid Systems and was assured by Technical Sales Director
Ramesh Khanna that the "full N+1 configuration would be incorporated into the purchase
order." The purchase order HYDC-01-PO-EL-002 was signed on 2 September 2025 with
explicit N+1 requirements incorporated into the PO commercial terms.

At the time, everyone believed the issue was resolved.

It was not.

CHAPTER 3 — THE SPECIFICATION MATURES (October 2025)
=====================================================

While procurement was being concluded, Rahul Desai and the Jacobs team were finalising
the technical specifications. A critical question came from Sanjay Thakur at WSP Global,
the independent commissioning authority: what exactly did "15-minute battery autonomy" mean?

Sanjay raised RFI-EL-002 on 15 October 2025. The answer would shape everything:

"The 15-minute autonomy requirement applies at end-of-life battery condition (80% of
rated capacity) at 25°C ambient temperature."

This was a significant clarification. Many vendors would calculate autonomy at new battery
condition (100% capacity). Calculating at end-of-life meant vendors would need to oversize
their battery systems to still meet 15 minutes when batteries were aging.

Rahul Desai incorporated this into Specification Rev B on 22 October 2025.
When PowerGrid Systems received Revision B, their application engineer Shalini Bose
ran the battery calculations. The numbers did not work.

The Vertiv Liebert EXL S1 system, as PowerGrid Systems had configured it, would provide
approximately 10 minutes at 100% IT load at end-of-life. To achieve 15 minutes, they
would need to add battery extension cabinets — at additional cost that had not been
in their tender price.

At this point, Shalini Bose and Ramesh Khanna faced a decision. They could:
a) Formally declare the deviation, risk losing the order to ABB Power Protection, and
   renegotiate pricing
b) Submit the technical submittal without declaring the deviation, hoping no one would
   notice until site commissioning when the battery test might be "adjusted"

They chose option (b).

CHAPTER 4 — THE SUBMITTAL AND THE REVIEW (November 2025)
=========================================================

On 30 October 2025, PowerGrid Systems submitted their technical submittal — Revision 01.
The deviation schedule on the last page read: "No deviations declared."

Kavya Reddy at Tata Projects' QA team received the submittal. She noticed immediately
that the Modbus register map required by REQ-UPS-013 was missing. She also noticed
that the IEC 62040-3 performance classification was not confirmed with a type test
certificate. She raised RFI-EL-004, formally rejecting Revision 01.

Revision 02 arrived on 18 November 2025. The Modbus register map was now included
(registers 1000-1015, as eventually confirmed in Section 5 of the submittal).
The IEC 62040-3 certificate was attached. The deviation schedule still read:
"No deviations declared."

This time, Kavya Reddy escalated to Rahul Desai for a full technical review.
Rahul worked through the submittal section by section against the specification.
Within two hours, he had identified five deviations:

1. N-only vs N+1 configuration (CRITICAL)
2. 10-minute autonomy vs 15-minute requirement (CRITICAL)
3. ±2 Hz bypass sync window vs ±1 Hz specified (MAJOR)
4. Battery discharge test proposed for site vs factory (MAJOR)
5. Battery conductance monitoring not in base scope (MINOR)

On 20 November 2025, Kavya Reddy raised Non-Conformance Report NCR-EL-001.
It was the most important quality document of the project to that point.

PowerGrid Systems received the NCR. Ramesh Khanna called Aditya Singh and argued
that the deviations were "minor technical points" and the equipment was "essentially
compliant." This argument was firmly rejected.

But nothing was formally acknowledged in writing.

CHAPTER 5 — THE SWITCHGEAR COMPLICATION (December 2025)
========================================================

While the UPS battle continued, a second crisis was developing.

The 11 kV medium voltage switchgear panels (Package PKG-EL-001, PO HYDC-01-PO-EL-001)
had been manufactured by Schneider Electric India at their Vadodara facility. On
28 November 2025, Schneider confirmed manufacturing was complete and the panels were
ready for dispatch. This was on schedule.

Then the panels were trucked to JNPT Mumbai for port clearance. What should have been
a routine customs process became a bureaucratic nightmare. The IGCR (Import General
Manifest Certificate) listed the equipment under an incorrect HS code. The customs
clearance was frozen on 5 December 2025.

Deepak Nambiar, Site Engineer, raised RFI-PROC-001 on 10 December 2025. The original
delivery date to site was 20 December 2025. Every day of delay was a day off the
critical path.

By 15 December 2025, Schneider's logistics team was estimating clearance on 10 January 2026.
Site delivery: 15-17 January 2026. A 25-27 day delay on the critical path.

Rajan Mehta updated the programme. Power-On moved from 30 June 2026 to 25 July 2026.
The client's bank customer's go-live date was now dangerously close to the boundary.

Simultaneously, another issue had emerged: Schneider Electric was offering to supply
the switchgear with a 25 kA short-circuit rating instead of the specified 31.5 kA.
The 31.5 kA panels would take 6 additional weeks to manufacture. Rahul Desai needed
to urgently assess whether 25 kA was acceptable.

He raised this as RFI-EL-003 on 22 November 2025. As of January 2026, it remained open.
Every week it stayed open, the switchgear delivery was at risk of slipping further.

CHAPTER 6 — THE FAT (January 2026)
====================================

On 5-7 January 2026, the UPS Factory Acceptance Test was conducted at PowerGrid Systems'
test facility in Pune.

The first two days went reasonably well. Load bank tests passed. Bypass transfer tests
confirmed a transfer time of less than 4 milliseconds. But the synchronisation window
was measured at ±2.1 Hz — exceeding the ±1 Hz specified in REQ-UPS-009. Sanjay Thakur
(WSP Global, attending as commissioning authority) noted this formally in his FAT observation
log but allowed the test to continue since the bypass transfer was successful under
controlled conditions.

On the morning of 7 January 2026, the battery discharge test began. Module UPS-A1-01
was loaded to 1,600 kW using the facility's load banks. The EnerSys battery monitoring
system showed the battery discharge rate.

At 9 minutes and 47 seconds, the battery terminal voltage dropped below the minimum
threshold. The UPS automatically transferred to bypass to protect the connected load.

The room was quiet.

Dr. Meera Iyer, representing NxtGen Cloud, looked at the test data logger printout.
The specification required 15 minutes. The measured result was 9 minutes 47 seconds.
The shortfall was 5 minutes 13 seconds — a 35% deficit.

She turned to Ramesh Khanna and said: "This equipment is not leaving your facility
until this is resolved. I will not authorise dispatch."

The test was formally recorded as FAILED. Sanjay Thakur issued a formal FAT failure
notification the same day (Action 13-10 from MOM-HYDC01-013).

CHAPTER 7 — THE MEETING (January 2026)
========================================

On 8 January 2026, the Weekly Project Coordination Meeting No. 13 took place.

Vikram Sharma opened the meeting. The atmosphere was tense. Two major procurement
issues — UPS battery failure and switchgear customs delay — were both on the agenda.

Sanjay Thakur presented the FAT results. The room absorbed the news.

Arjun Kapoor, who had flown up from Mumbai specifically for this meeting, spoke:
"Let me be very clear. Our customer — a major Indian bank — has signed contracts
based on a 1 September 2026 go-live date. We have a 25 July 2026 Power-On target,
giving us a 37-day buffer. If the UPS battery redesign takes six to eight weeks,
that buffer disappears. I want a cure notice drafted for PowerGrid Systems within
48 hours, and I want a battery remediation proposal from them by 19 January 2026."

Kavya Reddy was given the task.

Aditya Singh issued a formal hold on UPS equipment dispatch that same afternoon.
The equipment would not move from PowerGrid Systems' Pune facility until the
battery issue was resolved.

The project was at its most critical junction.
